Point 1: BrightnessBoost -- "Real CRT circuits compensate for this loss by increasing drive voltage"

Verdict: You are correct. The documentation wording is wrong.

CRT circuits do not automatically increase drive voltage to compensate for scanline gaps. The electron gun's drive voltage is determined by the video signal level, period. The correct explanation is:

On a real CRT, the Gaussian beam profile has tails that spread beyond the intended scanline, so adjacent scanlines physically overlap. The "dark gaps" between scanlines are less pronounced than in a discrete digital simulation. Our BrightnessBoost is a perceptual compensation to match what a real CRT's natural beam spread achieves -- this is standard practice in CRT shaders (CRT-Royale, CRT-Geom, etc. all use a similar brightness factor). The documentation will be corrected to reflect this.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Point 2: I/Q bandwidth asymmetry -- "no they don't (since the 1960s)"

Verdict: You are correct for practical consumer receivers.

The original 1953 NTSC standard (FCC Title 47 sec.73.682) specified I: 0-1.3 MHz and Q: 0-0.5 MHz bandwidth. However, as Charles Poynton documents in "Digital Video and HD: Algorithms and Interfaces" (2nd ed., sec.22.5), by the early 1960s virtually all consumer TV sets switched to symmetric quadrature demodulation with ~0.5 MHz bandwidth for both chroma axes, aligned with B-Y/R-Y rather than I/Q. The asymmetric I/Q demodulation was more expensive to implement and provided minimal perceptual benefit.

Our code uses kWinI=18, kWinQ=54 (I wider, Q narrower), which matches the 1953 standard but NOT what any NES-era consumer TV actually did. For accuracy to what NES games looked like on 1985-1995 TVs, symmetric chroma bandwidth would be more correct. Blargg's widely-used NES NTSC filter also uses symmetric bandwidth.

However: the transmitted NTSC composite waveform from the NES PPU is the same regardless of how the receiver decodes it. Our waveform generation is correct; the asymmetry is in the decoder stage only, and affects the visual result subtly (slightly different color fringing patterns).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Point 3: Interlace Field Jitter on 240p -- "The CRT doesn't understand that the video is interlaced or not"

Verdict: You are correct.

The NES outputs 240p (262 lines/frame, non-interlaced). In a progressive signal, every frame starts at the same vertical position -- there are no alternating fields with half-scanline offsets. The CRT vertical deflection circuit simply follows the sync pulses, which are identical every frame for 240p. There is no physical mechanism for "inter-field jitter" on a progressive signal.

Our own internal documentation already noted this inconsistency: "NES native 240p doesn't do interlacing." The InterlaceJitter feature will be re-documented as a purely aesthetic/artistic effect, not a hardware simulation.

Regarding "breathing": you correctly identify that any real slow visual oscillation would be due to a beat frequency between the mains power (60 Hz) and the NES vsync rate (60.0988 Hz), which produces a ~17-second beat pattern. This is the "hum bar" effect, not interlace jitter.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Point 4: Phosphor Persistence -- "so many orders of magnitude dimmer"

Verdict: You are correct regarding the magnitude.

P22 phosphor (standard for color CRT TVs) has these approximate persistence times to 10% brightness:
- P22 Red (Y2O2S:Eu): ~1-3 ms
- P22 Green (ZnS:Cu,Al): ~1 ms
- P22 Blue (ZnS:Ag): ~20-60 us

At 60 Hz NTSC, the frame period is 16.7 ms. After one full frame period, even the slowest P22 component has decayed to well below 1% -- essentially invisible. Our implementation with PhosphorDecay = 0.6 (60% carryover per frame) is vastly exaggerated compared to real P22 physics.

The phosphor persistence in our emulator is an aesthetic effect that some users enjoy (similar to the "ghosting" option in many CRT shaders), but the documentation should not claim it's a faithful reproduction of real P22 phosphor behavior. It will be re-labeled as an artistic enhancement.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Point 5: Beam Convergence -- "On a good TV this will have been compensated for"

Verdict: Partially correct, but nuanced.

Quality CRT TVs did have static and dynamic convergence adjustments, and a well-calibrated set would show minimal convergence error. However:

- Many cheap consumer TVs (the kind most NES players used) had mediocre convergence, especially at edges/corners
- Convergence degraded over time as components aged
- Even well-adjusted TVs typically showed 0.5-2mm misconvergence at screen edges (per SMPTE RP 167 acceptable tolerances)
- Service manuals for 1980s-1990s consumer TVs (RCA, Zenith, Sony) all include convergence adjustment procedures, precisely because it was a common real-world issue

Our default ConvergenceStrength = 2.0 pixels is on the high side for a well-maintained TV but reasonable for an aged or cheap set. Making it adjustable (including 0 = off) is the right approach.
