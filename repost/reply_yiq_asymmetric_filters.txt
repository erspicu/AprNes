The question touches on a key distinction: NTSC I/Q vs. PAL-style symmetric U/V.

The NTSC standard intentionally specifies asymmetric bandwidths for the two chrominance axes:

- I channel: 1.3 MHz bandwidth (in our implementation: 18-tap Hann window at 21.477 MHz -> ~1.3 MHz)
- Q channel: 0.4 MHz bandwidth (54-tap Hann window -> ~0.4 MHz)

This isn't an arbitrary choice -- it's the actual NTSC spec (SMPTE 170M / FCC 73.682). The I axis was deliberately rotated 33 degrees from the (R-Y) axis to align with the orange-cyan range (skin tones, earth tones), where human vision has higher chroma acuity. The Q axis (magenta-green) falls in a range where the eye is far less sensitive, so it gets a narrower bandwidth.

You're correct that most consumer TVs took a shortcut: they demodulated at 0 and 90 degrees (essentially recovering B-Y and R-Y, i.e., U and V) with two identical ~0.5 MHz filters. This is cheaper to build -- symmetric filters, no 33-degree phase offset -- and "good enough" for most viewing. This is sometimes called "wideband I" or "symmetric decoding."

But since we're simulating the signal path in software with no hardware cost constraints, we chose to implement spec-correct I/Q demodulation with the proper asymmetric bandwidths. The result is:

1. More color detail along the I axis -- you get 1.3 MHz of chroma resolution in the skin-tone direction, vs. 0.4-0.5 MHz with symmetric decoding
2. Correct chroma fringing behavior -- the different filter widths produce the characteristic asymmetric color bleeding that differs from a symmetric decoder
3. Historically accurate for higher-end NTSC monitors and professional equipment that did implement true I/Q decoding

Since we're already running a 21.477 MHz time-domain simulation with per-sample FIR convolution, the cost of using two different filter lengths is negligible -- it's just a wider kernel for Q (54 vs 18 taps). There's no reason to compromise to the symmetric shortcut that only existed because of analog hardware cost.
